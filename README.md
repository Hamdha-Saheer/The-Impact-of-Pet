Project Overview
This project explores the human-animal bond's impact on mental health within an urban Sri Lankan context. By analyzing data collected from 126 pet owners, we utilized R to investigate whether higher levels of pet attachment correlate with reduced perceived stress.

Technical Workflow in R
Data Processing: Cleaned and transformed raw survey data using R, including handling missing values and categorizing demographic variables.

Statistical Testing:

Chi-Square Analysis: Used to compare stress levels across different pet types and attachment levels.

Pearson Correlation: Calculated the relationship between the Comfort from Companion Animal Scale (CCAS) and the Perceived Stress Scale (PSS).

Linear Regression: Modeled the predictive power of CCAS scores on PSS scores.

Visualization: Generated density plots, scatter plots, and regression lines to visualize the distribution of stress across the sample population.

Core Findings
Stress Distribution: The analysis revealed a statistically significant bias toward medium stress levels (p = 0.0000) among young adults in the Western Province.

Attachment Impact: While qualitative data suggests pets provide emotional comfort, the quantitative R model showed that pet interaction alone was not a statistically significant predictor of stress relief in this specific demographic.

Complexity: The results suggest that external socio-economic factors likely play a more dominant role in stress levels than pet ownership for this age group (12–36 years).
